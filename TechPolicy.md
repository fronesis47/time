## Background and First Principle

We now have access to extensive research that repeatedly demonstrates that not only smartphones but also any device connected to an updated “feed” with notifications proves to be an enormous distraction that prevents even the most diligent from focusing. “Multitasking” has proven to be a myth and even the visible presence of phones degrades intellectual performance. The starting point, therefore, for any environment of teaching and learning must be the elimination of all such devices. Accordingly, **our first principle is pen/pencil and paper only (P&P).**

## Clarification on the Usefulness of Networked Devices

None of the above should be understood as a blunt “anti-technology” stance, nor taken as denial that digital technology and electronic networked devices can, in certain contexts, prove to be enormously useful. Usefulness depends on *context*, however. In most cases the use of networked computing devices degrades the learning environment much more than it enhances it. Laptops and even phones can and have been integrated into the classroom in myriad ways, and certainly some things can be done with this tech that cannot be done without it. Nonetheless, with almost twenty years of data, we now know that the utility of this tech comes with massive downsides that almost always outweigh the benefits. Hence our commitment to the first principle: P&P.

## Exceptions

When it comes to the second part of our prime directive (paper), digital technology has already revolutionized our access to written information. A single Kindle can easily “hold” 1,000 books, and a PDF reader or tablet allows interactions with written text that both includes what we can do with printed paper and in some ways goes beyond it. In order to make it possible for us to gain the obvious benefits of digital tech, while still trying to avoid the dangers of networked devices, you will have two options in this class. First, you can stick to P&P, printing out your reading assignments, marking them up with pencil, highlighter, or pen, and bringing them to class. ### Final Note on Tech *Outside* the Classroom

I am a scholar and a teacher, not a cop. It is neither my job nor my calling to police the behavior of students outside the classroom. However, I strongly recommend against the use of LLMs: if you do not think for yourself in this class, you will neither learn anything nor excel in the class.
