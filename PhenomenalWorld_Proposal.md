
### Overview

Political economy today is haunted by the specter of gold. Across a variety of disciplines in the social sciences and humanities, writings on money, finance, and the monetary economy remain severely hamstrung by the myth of gold. This is the simple but abiding idea that once upon a time, *gold really was money*.

The 2020s have witnessed a nascent quasi-consensus in theories of money, with the very best scholarship (by sociologists, economists, political scientists, anthropologists, and others) affirming contemporary money as so-called “fiat currency.” For some, that phrase means tokens of credit/debt, circulated by banks and other financial institutions; for others, it means *charta*, symbols of monetary value brought into being by state declaration. Yet even the best of this work tacitly accepts the idea that *in the past* (and a not-so-distant past, at that) silver and gold were themselves money.

Much like the myth of barter, which previously plagued both scholarly and popular understandings of how political economy works – that is, how economic forces and relations structure, shape, constrain, and enable political, social, and cultural possibilities within a society – today the myth of gold sets significant limits on both our ability to make sense of credit, debt, capital, and finance and our capacity to imagine or bring about transformed financial relations. Our impoverished “money imaginary” cuts us off from any hope for more democratic monetary policy.

This book will do for the myth of gold what David Graeber did for the myth of barter: permanently vanquish it. To prove that gold was *never* money, we cannot merely rely on logical arguments about the necessary relation between the price of metal bullion and the denomination of coins (comprised of that metal). Rather, we have to tackle the hardest cases, to take on the historical instances in which silver and gold appear to be money itself. *Bright and Shiny Things* turns to money market examples from seventeenth- to early twentieth-century Britain, not to tell a historical story but to prove a crucial conceptual point: the very nature of money is such that gold can never be, and never was, money.

To vanquish the myth of gold is to prove beyond all doubt that there is no such thing as “stable” money; rather, the search for stable money is always a political and economic tragedy. Money is a *measure* of value, but it is not a *standard* of value. Neither a bar of gold, nor a barrel of oil, nor a “bitcoin” can ever be money. This means, in turn, that money has always been “financialized”: from the fifteenth century foreign bill of exchange to a contemporary credit default swap, money relations always involve speculation on future value.


### Audience, Competition, and Marketing

This book participates in a vibrant conversation, and contributes to a now-rich literature, on both the theory of money, and the contested histories and politics of money that develop out of those various theories. Given the continued growing interest in money theory and money politics – as we see evidenced by recent, wildly popular books like David McWilliams’s *The History of Money* (Simon & Schuster, 2025) – this book has a ready-made audience. Having already established myself as a key contributor to these debates, a large group of readers will be anticipating the publication of this new book. More than merely a “contribution,” however, this book has the potential to change the conversation and make a definitive impact not just on contemporary debates, but on our entire understanding of the history of money. While this book speaks to an extant literature, it makes a *unique* contribution by attempting what has never been tried before: to use credit theory to explain the cases of coinage – the cases that pose the most serious challenge. 

The comparison to Graeber’s *Debt* (Melville House, 2011) is highly ambitious, of course, but I contend that it is also apt: the subtitle of that book “The First 5,000 Years” suggests not just a history, but – in a book that runs to well over 500 pages – an especially long and dry one at that. Yet Graeber’s book was transformative because his conceptual and historical work led to a very focused point about the political and moral obligations of debt. Perhaps the more direct comparison would be to Christine Desan’s major work, *Making Money* (Oxford 2014), another text that focuses on historical cases, but is constantly cited for its contribution to our understanding of monetary policy (in the form of neo-chartalism). 

*Bright and Shiny Things* will not only definitively expose the idea of gold-as-money for the myth that it is but also reshape how we think about money and finance in the present. To understand the seventeenth-century English shilling as a complexly engineered financial asset – a callable consol with an in-built derivative – is to force us to rethink the entire dominant narrative of “financialization” as a phenomenon that began in the 1970s.  

And this means that the potential audience for this book proves much broader than just those already participating in the debates over the history and theory of money, because I draw from an array of interdisciplinary sources and consistently build bridges to a diverse set of readers and literatures. This includes heterodox economics, interdisciplinary finance studies, and critical historical political economy. My recent work – including presentations of early versions of the material in this book – has resonated with all three of these groups. My 2023 book was featured as the centerpiece of a lengthy review essay in the *Cambridge Journal of Economics*; I published a lead article in *Finance and Society* (itself following a keynote of the Finance and Society Network conference in London); and I recently gave talks on this new material at CUNY and Columbia. Concretely, this means the book will not only intervene consequentially in the ongoing conversations about monetary policy, monetary history, and money markets, but also significantly widen the conversation itself – for example, by speaking powerfully to readers of Stanford’s vibrant series, [*Currencies*](https://www.sup.org/books/series/currencies-new-thinking-financial-times), appealing to students and followers of Perry Mehrling’s [money view](https://omny.fm/shows/odd-lots/perry-mehrling-explains-why-the-money-view-is-key), or making a distinctive contribution to our understanding of historical instruments of finance like bills of exchange.

In the context of audience and market, I wish to underscore that while earlier books of mine followed the academic genre of speaking to a presumed disciplinary audience, my most recent two books aim for a much wider and more general audience. *Capitalist Economics* ‌(Oxford 2022) both targets an audience of students from any discipline (with no prior knowledge of economics of politics) and also speaks to a wider public readership interested in the fundamental question of how capitalism works. It offers readers an introduction to capitalist economics, as an alternative to (not a critique of) the neoclassical paradigm of modern economics as it is taught to tens of thousands of intro students all across the globe. *Money Has No Value* (De Gruyter, 2023) is a much more intellectual or scholarly work, but still not at all a narrowly disciplinary academic text. It too presupposes no prior knowledge and strives for clarity and concision. In both of these prior recent works, as with this proposed book, my method is not to take sophisticated ideas and dumb them down, but rather to first *teach* my readers what they need to know in order to engage fully with those complex ideas. 

For the past two years I have been more self-consciously building an audience for this book through my substack newsletter, “Money/Power.” Since February 2024 I have published approximately 35 pieces of 2k-5k words each, and during that time I have seen slow but very steady growth. I currently have a subscriber base of nearly 450 (including a small portion of paid subscribers), and I see strong numbers for engagement with my posts on the site and “percentage opened” stats for email distribution of the newsletter. This would serve as an obvious platform to help launch this new book. Finally, with the creation at Johns Hopkins of a variety of new interdisciplinary “political economy” initiatives, I currently have funding earmarked to sponsor an international political economy conference. This is, as yet, unscheduled, but it could easily be timed to help support the launch of the book.

### Author Information

I am Professor and former Chair of the Department of Political Science at Johns Hopkins University. I have authored eight books across a wide range of topics in the social sciences and humanities, all centered on the combined conceptual and historical work that reflects my education as an interdisciplinary political theorist. For the past decade my writings have focused intensely on the vibrant contemporary debates around the theory and history of money, with my *Money Has No Value* (de Gruyter 2023) making a decisive intervention in those debates and helping to establish the credit theory of money as the most powerful tool for doing rigorous analysis of money theory, history, and politics.
